Projection

Projections involves projectors starting from 3-D points and hitting the 2-D projection place, forming the image of the point.

Two types of projections:

  1. Parallel projection: Projectors are parallel to each other.

  1. Perspective projection: All projects pass through a point. This is similar to pinhole camera. Primarily, we use these projections for our work.

Parallel Projections

  1. Orthographic: Projection plane is perpendicular to the direction of projection.
    • Lengths parallel to the projection plane are preserved.
    • Only direction of projection matters, distance from the point to the projection plane doesn't.
    • Good approximation for a camera with long focal length.
  1. Oblique: Otherwise.

Volume of Visibility

Cameras have finite fields of view in horizontal and vertical directions.

The shape of visible space is a cylinder for orthographic projections and a cone starting from the CoP (Center of Projection) for perspective seems natural.

Mathematics is harder for cones which is why we use rectangular structures.

View Volume: The volume of potentially visible space.

View Volume

View volume is a cube for orthographics cameras and a (truncated) pyramid for perspective projections.

4 planes (left, right, top, bottom) define the view volume.

Graphics cameras use 2 additional planes to limit visibility: near, far

Planes are specified in VRC, they move with the camera.

What about the focal length

An ideal pinhole camera has the whole world in focus

Finite focal-length lenses introduce the effect of focus in real cameras.

Even for them, the depth of field (region in focus) increases as the f-stop increases or the aperture gets smaller.

Computer Graphics simulates ideal pin-hole cameras.

Depth of field can be simulated by intentional blurring.

View volume is specified by 6 planes: left, right, top, bottom, near, far. All values in VRC.
